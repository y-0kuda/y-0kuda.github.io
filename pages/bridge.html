Bridge Engineer

My Experience

I worked as a bridge engineer in Cebu, Philippines, from August 2022 to December 2024. In this article, I will summarize what I learned from that experience.

Job Description

A bridge engineer acts as an intermediary between the programming team and the business team that defines the specifications of web services. The role involves managing project progress and handling inquiries from both sides.

You might wonder why the programmers and the business team don't communicate directly. While that is possible, there are two main reasons why bridge engineers are needed.

First, it allows both sides to focus on their core responsibilities. Programmers are responsible for writing code, and the business team is responsible for defining specifications. Tasks like progress management and handling inquiries are not part of their primary roles. By taking on these responsibilities, a bridge engineer reduces their workload.

Second, a bridge engineer helps prevent miscommunication. When the programming team and the business team are from different countries, misunderstandings can easily occur. In my case, the programmers were Filipino and the business team was Japanese. Even if both sides communicate in English, subtle differences in nuance can lead to mismatched expectations. This can result in a product that differs from what the business team originally envisioned. A bridge engineer fills this gap by ensuring that the developed service meets the business requirements.

Whether a bridge engineer reads code or conducts testing depends on the company. In my case, I was involved in both. Overall, the role involves supporting both sides while managing progress and communication.

Pros and Cons

This role is well suited for people who enjoy communicating with individuals from other countries and are comfortable using English or other foreign languages. It also helps if you are open to learning programming skills.

On the other hand, it may not be suitable for those who prefer working independently without much communication or who do not enjoy interacting with people from different cultures.

Required skills

Proficiency in a language other than your native one, such as English, is essential. Programming skills are not strictly required at the beginning, as they can be learned on the job.

In my case, I had a decent level of English but no professional programming experience when I was hired. My skills were at the level of being able to create a simple to-do list using PHP.

Applying for a job

If you search for "bridge engineer" on job sites like Indeed, you can find many opportunities. In my case, I found over 500 listings from Japanese companies.

It's important to keep applying regardless of whether you think you'll be accepted or not. Creating a simple web service as a portfolio and being able to explain what you built will help. You should also clearly state your language proficiency on your resume. I included the results of English tests I had taken.

If you get an interview, focus on demonstrating your ability to communicate in a foreign language, your basic programming knowledge, and what you find appealing about the company you applied to.

Summary

I would highly recommend that people who enjoy speaking foreign languages—especially English—or communicating with people from different countries consider becoming a bridge engineer.

This role allows you to develop your skills while working in the ever-growing IT industry and doing something you enjoy. It's also a good option for those who find it challenging to spend all their time writing code. While you may occasionally read code, the role includes a variety of other tasks.

You can improve your programming skills while communicating with people overseas and earning a salary. If you're interested, it's definitely worth exploring further.
